Quantitative and Dynamic Plant Ecology

Biology students looking for a route into the massive literature on plant community structure and vegetation analysis will find this third edition of Kershaw and Looney's book a godsend. First published in 1964, the book is an unusual combination of overviews of current theoretical issues and explanations of common field and analytical methods used in the description of plant communities. The newly expanded chapter integrating ideas of succession and climax that underlie much of our thinking about vegetation structure is particularly well done. This juxtaposition of theory and methodology emphasizes the role of theory in our predisposition toward particular methods and likewise the influence of methodology on our interpretation of the validity of theory. Emphasis is on the description of patterns in vegetation with chapters on "The Causal Factors of Positive and Negative Association between Species," "The Poisson Series and the Detection of Non-randomness," "The Causal Factors of Pattern," "The Detection of Natural Groupings of Species: Classification Methods," and three chapters on "'Ordination Methods.'" A chapter on "Plant Population Dynamics" highlights studies of population level consequences of patterns in plant germination, establishment, and growth, although for the most part the overview of these topics is left to other publications. The discussions are clearly written and profusely illustrated with examples including tables and figures from the literature. Although not a "cook book" of analytical methods, the book describes techniques in sufficient detail for the reader to gain some appreciation of the advantages and limitations of many currently in use. The literature on ordination techniques is particularly large and diffuse; variations on methodological themes abound. Thus the chapters comparing classification and ordination techniques will be particularly welcome to those contemplating the analysis of a large data set. However, I was dismayed to see continuum analysis (also called polar or Bray-Curtis ordination) dismissed as "crude and very unwieldy" (p. 198) when in fact it has been shown to be one of the more intuitively simple, robust, and interpretable methods available. Several of the more recently developed techniques are applied to a single field data set (a Hudson's Bay salt marsh) providing a valuable comparison of their relative capacities for elucidating or distorting pattern. Two complete data sets for the reader's use are included in the appendix. This new edition of the familiar Kershaw and Looney will be a welcome introduction and source of information for all those ecologists who find a need to describe and compare the plant communities in which they work..
